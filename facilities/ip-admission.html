IP Admission

  • IP (in-patient) admission usually involves admitting a patient for an extended stay to receive dental treatment and care within the hospital's dental department. Unlike outpatient care where patients visit for routine procedures and leave the same day.
  • IP admission is typically required for more complex dental surgeries, treatments, or cases where ongoing monitoring and recovery in a hospital setting are necessary.

IP Admission in NABH Dental Hospital For:

1. Maxillofacial Trauma Management

2. Complex & Pediatric Dental Procedures under GA or Nitrous Oxide

3. Implant Surgery (Advanced/Full-Arch)

4. Medically Compromised Patients for Dental Extraction

Maxillofacial Trauma Management

Includes:

  • Fractures of mandible, zygoma, maxilla, orbital floor
  • Road traffic accidents, sports injuries
  • Soft tissue laceration, dental avulsion with facial injuries

Why IP?

  • Requires GA, ORIF, or closed reduction
  • Airway risks, bleeding, infection control
  • Often requires ICU monitoring post-op

Pediatric Dental Procedures (GA/Nitrous Oxide)

Examples:

  • Special needs children (autism, cerebral palsy)
  • Extensive caries/full-mouth rehabilitation
  • Pulp therapy, stainless steel crowns under sedation

Why IP?

  • General anesthesia or deep sedation risks
  • Need for recovery monitoring, behavior observation
  • Risk of aspiration, airway compromise, delayed emergence

Implant Surgeries (Complex Cases)

Examples:

  • Full-arch rehabilitation (All-on-4, zygomatic implants)
  • Simultaneous sinus lift or bone grafting
  • Implants in medically compromised or geriatric patients

Why IP?

  • GA (General Anesthesia) or IV sedation commonly used
  • Risk of excessive bleeding, sinus perforation, swelling
  • Need for post-op pain management, IV antibiotics

Dental Extraction in Medically Compromised Patients

Patients may have:

  • Cardiac history (recent MI, pacemaker, anticoagulants)
  • Uncontrolled diabetes
  • Renal disease/dialysis patients
  • Bleeding disorders (hemophilia, low platelets)
  • Cancer patients (on chemo/radiation)

Why IP?

  • Higher risk of bleeding, delayed healing
  • Need for medical monitoring, physician collaboration
  • In some cases, bridging therapy for anticoagulants required
